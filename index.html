God With Us

Encountering Jesus in the Gospel of Matthew

A Deep Dive into the World of the Messiah-King, based on the work of Edward Sri

Introduction: The Lens for a King

Understanding Why Jesus Lived

Before we can even begin to grasp the earth-shattering meaning of Jesus's death and resurrection, we must first immerse ourselves in the world he entered. The central question of this study is not *how* Jesus died, but *why he lived*. The Gospel of Matthew, more than any other, serves as our guide. It was written for a Jewish-Christian audience, steeped in the stories, prophecies, and hopes of the Old Testament. To read Matthew is to see Jesus through their eyes.

The interpretive key Matthew gives us is the name Emmanuel, which means "God with us." This is the core of Jesus's mission: to restore the intimate communion between God and humanity that was lost to sin. He is not just a great teacher or a miracle worker; he is the long-awaited Messiah-King, the fulfillment of the Davidic royal line, come to establish his everlasting kingdom. Matthew meticulously, like a master craftsman, demonstrates how every detail of Jesus's life—from his birth to his final breath—is a fulfillment of God's ancient promises.

Part I: The King's Identity and Mission

Chapter 1: The Return of the King (Matthew 1 )

Decoding the Royal Birth Certificate

Most modern readers skip genealogies. To us, they seem like a dry, dusty list of names. But for a first-century Jew, the genealogy in Matthew 1 was a breathtaking headline. It was a royal proclamation, a divine birth certificate proving that God's plan of salvation was reaching its climax.

Matthew immediately identifies Jesus as the "Son of David," the rightful heir to Israel's throne. But he doesn't stop there. He structures the entire genealogy into three sets of fourteen generations. Why fourteen? This is a masterful Hebrew literary device called *gematria*, where letters have numerical values. The name "David" (D-W-D in Hebrew) adds up to 14 (D=4, W=6, D=4). Matthew is shouting through this structure: Jesus is the "thrice-Davidic" king, the ultimate fulfillment of the royal line!

The genealogy's crucial turning point is the Babylonian Exile. This wasn't just a distant memory; it was the defining trauma of the Jewish people, marking the end of their kingdom. They were a people in waiting, longing for a new king to end their long, dark night of oppression. The genealogy builds a palpable tension, moving through the exile and then, finally, to its stunning conclusion: Jesus, the "Christ" (the anointed Messiah). His very name, *Jesus* ("God saves"), is the answer to the problem of the exile, which at its root was caused by sin. He has come to save his people from their sins and end their true, spiritual exile from God.

Modern Comparison: The Uncovered Constitution

Imagine a people living under a dictatorship for centuries, their original founding documents thought lost forever. One day, an archaeologist uncovers the original constitution, and it contains a secret clause that points to a specific person, alive today, as the one destined to restore freedom. The genealogy in Matthew 1 is that document. It's not just a family tree; it's the legal and divine basis for a revolution of hope.

Chapter 2: Fulfilling All Prophecy (Matthew 2)

History as a Divine Echo

In this chapter, Matthew acts as a master teacher, showing his students how all of history was echoing and pointing toward Jesus. He uses two powerful teaching tools. The first is explicit fulfillment quotations, where he literally stops the story to say, "This happened to fulfill what was spoken by the prophet..." and then provides the Old Testament quote. It's like a professor pointing directly to a textbook citation to prove a point.

His second, more subtle tool is typology. This is where an Old Testament person, event, or institution becomes a "type" or prefigurement of something greater in the New Testament. Matthew presents Jesus as the fulfillment of these types:

  • Jesus as the New Moses: Herod's decree to kill the male infants in Bethlehem is a chilling echo of Pharaoh's decree against the Hebrew baby boys. Jesus's escape to Egypt and his later return to Israel perfectly mirror the life of Moses. Matthew is showing us that Jesus is the new, ultimate liberator and lawgiver, who will lead his people not out of Egypt, but out of the slavery of sin.
  • The Magi and the Echo of Balaam: The story of the wise men from the East is a "remake" of a famous Old Testament story from Numbers 22-24. There, a wicked king (Balak) hires a pagan seer from the East (Balaam) to curse Israel. But God intervenes, and Balaam ends up blessing Israel and prophesying that "a star shall come out of Jacob, and a scepter shall rise out of Israel." Now, centuries later, a new wicked king (Herod) tries to use wise men from the East for his own destructive purpose, but they too end up honoring the true king, whose coming is announced by a star. The echo would have been unmistakable to Matthew's audience.

Conclusion: The Invitation of the King

What Lessons Does the Author Want Us to Draw?

Having journeyed through the rich, layered world of Matthew's Gospel, we arrive at the essential lessons Edward Sri wants us to carry into our own lives. This book is not an academic exercise; it is an invitation to a deeper relationship with Jesus Christ, our King. The lessons are transformative:

Core Lessons for a Modern Disciple:

  • Read the Bible in 3D: The author's primary lesson is that we must stop reading the Bible in a flat, ahistorical way. To truly understand the New Testament, we must see it as the fulfillment of the Old. By learning the Jewish context, the stories, and the prophecies, the figure of Jesus leaps off the page in three dimensions, revealing the breathtaking coherence of God's plan across thousands of years. We are challenged to become better students of the *whole* story of salvation.
  • The Kingdom is an Inside Job: Many of Jesus's contemporaries wanted a political revolutionary. We, too, often look for external solutions to our problems. Sri shows us that Jesus's kingdom is first and foremost an internal one. It is a revolution of the heart. True freedom from the "oppressors" in our lives—be it anxiety, addiction, injustice, or fear—begins with surrendering to the reign of Christ within us. The kingdom's values of mercy, humility, and sacrificial love are the weapons that truly change the world.
  • You Are Part of the Story: The Great Commission was not just for the twelve apostles. By virtue of our baptism, we are made citizens of the kingdom and commissioned as its ambassadors. The author wants us to see that our daily lives—our interactions at work, our patience with our family, our service to the poor—are the modern-day battlegrounds where the kingdom is either advanced or hindered. We are not passive spectators; we are active participants in this divine drama.
  • Embrace the Promise of "Emmanuel": Perhaps the most crucial lesson is the most comforting: we are not alone. The promise that gives the book its title, "God with us," is the fuel for the Christian life. Sri emphasizes that Christ's presence is not a vague spiritual idea but a tangible reality. He is with us in the community of the Church, in the inspired words of Scripture, in our service to the marginalized, and most intimately and powerfully, in the Real Presence of the Eucharist. Our confidence and courage to live as kingdom citizens come from this unwavering promise.

Ultimately, the deep dive into Matthew's Gospel is a call to discipleship. It challenges us to move beyond a casual acquaintance with Jesus and to truly know him as our Messiah-King. It invites us to see our own lives as part of his grand, unfolding story of redeeming the world and to live each moment with the joyful confidence that "God is with us."
